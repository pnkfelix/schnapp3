CSG 2D — Solid / Anti-Solid Semantics

Two-dimensional cross-sections illustrating CSG operations. Replace "disk" with "ball" for ℝ³. All diagrams use the two-component (polarity, distance) model.

solid (+1) anti-solid (−1) empty (0)

In each diagram: solid outlines mark the boundary of the named shape; dashed outlines are reference-only (the shape exists but is not the primary actor).

1. The four regions of A and B

Two overlapping solid disks produce four distinct regions.

2. anti(B) — negative charge, same geometry

anti flips polarity but leaves the distance field unchanged. Anti-solid occupies the same region as B but has charge −1.

3. complement(B) — flipped geometry, same charge

complement negates the distance field of B: inside↔outside swap. The result is an infinite solid with a hole at B. A is not involved here at all.

4. Combining A with complement(B): union vs intersect

Now we introduce A. The two operators give very different results — union expands outward, intersect clips inward.

The contrast: union with an infinite solid gives you almost the whole plane; intersect clips it back down to the finite extent of A, minus B. This is why intersect is the right operator for scoped subtraction.

5. All combinations: {union, intersect} × {A, anti A, compl A, anti compl A} × {B, anti B, compl B, anti compl B}

Shorthand: cX = complement(X); acX = anti(complement(X)) = complement(anti(X)). The four variants of each operand:

X solid disk: polarity +1 inside, 0 outside
anti X anti-solid disk: polarity −1 inside, 0 outside (same geometry, opposite charge)
cX infinite solid with hole: polarity +1 outside X, 0 inside X
acX infinite anti-solid with hole: polarity −1 outside X, 0 inside X

Background color shows the outside-both region (infinite extent when non-gray). Hover a cell for the formula. Thin circle outlines show A (left) and B (right) for reference.
